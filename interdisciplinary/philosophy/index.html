Research Philosophy: Integration Through Questions

“The best science happens at the boundaries between disciplines, where different perspectives illuminate problems in unexpected ways.”

Core Principles

1. Question-Driven, Not Method-Driven

Traditional approach:

  • “I’m a microscopist, what can I study with microscopy?”
  • Technique dictates research direction

My approach:

  • “How do proteins access DNA in cells?”
  • Question dictates which techniques to integrate

Example from my research: Understanding RecA-RNA discrimination required:

  • FRET (physics/chemistry) for distance measurements
  • CoSMoS (biochemistry) for binding kinetics
  • Microfluidics (engineering) for controlled environments
  • Biological context (molecular biology) for functional interpretation

2. Physical Principles Guide Biological Understanding

The connection:

  • Biological systems obey physical laws
  • Physical constraints shape biological function
  • Understanding physics enables biological prediction

Key insights from my work:

DNA Confinement Physics ‚Üí Chromatin Accessibility

  • Physics: Polymer behavior under confinement (Odijk regime)
  • Biology: How cells package 2 meters of DNA into nucleus
  • Integration: Chromatin structure emerges from physical constraints

Molecular Crowding ‚Üí Reaction Kinetics

  • Physics: Excluded volume effects
  • Chemistry: Altered binding equilibria
  • Biology: Why cellular reactions differ from test tube experiments

Single-Molecule Methods ‚Üí Population Heterogeneity

  • Physics: Statistical mechanics of rare events
  • Biology: Cell-to-cell variability in gene expression
  • Integration: Understanding how rare events drive cellular decisions

3. Technology Development Enables Scientific Discovery

Philosophy: Investing in method development creates opportunities for discoveries impossible with existing tools.

Examples from my trajectory:

Phase 1: Developing Confinement Platforms (2008-2014)

Investment: Learning microfluidic fabrication, designing nanofluidic channels
Payoff: Ability to study DNA in physiologically relevant geometries
Discovery: Quantitative understanding of DNA behavior under confinement

Phase 2: Adapting Methods for Protein-DNA Studies (2011-2025)

Investment: Integrating microfluidics with single-molecule fluorescence
Payoff: Ability to study RecA with unprecedented control
Discovery: RNA discrimination mechanisms in recombination

Phase 3: Extending to Complex Systems (Current/Future)

Investment: Multi-reservoir systems for crowding + confinement
Payoff: Can now study p53-histone competition under physiological conditions
Expected discovery: Quantitative model of transcription factor accessibility

The pattern: Each technological capability builds on previous platforms, creating compounding returns on methodological investment.

4. Quantitative Frameworks Bridge Scales

The challenge: How to connect molecular measurements to cellular function?

My approach: Develop quantitative models that link:

  • Single-molecule parameters ‚Üí Population behavior
  • In vitro measurements ‚Üí In vivo predictions
  • Physical properties ‚Üí Biological outcomes

Example: From Kd to Cellular Function

Single-molecule measurement:
RecA-ssDNA Kd = 0.162 ŒºM
RecA-ssRNA Kd = 1.119 ŒºM

‚Üì Quantitative modeling

Population prediction:
At physiological RecA concentration (1 ŒºM),
~86% of ssDNA will be RecA-bound
~47% of ssRNA will be RecA-bound

‚Üì Biological interpretation

Functional consequence:
RecA preferentially processes DNA over RNA,
preventing inappropriate RNA-templated recombination

This quantitative chain connects molecular measurements to biological function.

Integration in Practice

How I Approach New Problems

Step 1: Define the Biological Question

  • What is the phenomenon?
  • Why is it important?
  • What don’t we understand?

Step 2: Identify Physical Principles

  • What physical factors are relevant?
  • What constraints exist?
  • What can physics predict?

Step 3: Select/Develop Appropriate Methods

  • What techniques can access the relevant scales?
  • Do existing methods suffice?
  • What new capabilities are needed?

Step 4: Design Experiments

  • How to isolate variables?
  • How to control for confounding factors?
  • How to validate findings?

Step 5: Build Quantitative Models

  • How to extract parameters?
  • How to test predictions?
  • How to connect to biological context?

Case Study: The p53-Histone Project

Applying integration philosophy to a new problem:

The Biological Question

How does p53 access its DNA binding sites when they’re packaged in chromatin?

Physical Principles at Play

  1. Molecular crowding: Excluded volume effects (300-400 mg/mL proteins in cells)
  2. Spatial confinement: Chromatin restricts DNA accessibility
  3. Competitive binding: p53 vs histones for same DNA sequence

Why This Integration is Unique

Most studies examine one factor:

  • Chromatin structure (pure biology)
  • p53 binding (pure biochemistry)
  • Molecular crowding (pure biophysics)

This project integrates all three:

  • How does crowding affect p53-histone competition?
  • How does confinement alter chromatin accessibility?
  • What are the quantitative rules governing p53 access?

Requires:

  • Physics background (confinement + crowding)
  • Biochemistry expertise (protein-DNA interactions)
  • Engineering skills (platform development)
  • Biological context (chromatin function)

Few researchers have this combination ‚Üí Unique opportunity to make discoveries impossible for single-discipline approaches.

Training Philosophy

For students/postdocs:

Breadth Before Depth

  • Exposure to multiple disciplines early
  • Understanding how different fields approach problems
  • Learning to translate between disciplinary languages

Depth Through Integration

  • Master specific techniques deeply
  • But always in service of broader questions
  • Technical expertise enables integration, not isolation

Collaborative Mindset

  • No one can be expert in everything
  • Effective collaboration requires:
    • Understanding collaborators’ perspectives
    • Speaking their language
    • Respecting different approaches

Measuring Success

How to evaluate integration?

Poor integration:

  • “I used technique X on system Y”
  • Technique and biology are disconnected
  • Results don’t inform understanding

Good integration:

  • “Physical principle X explains biological phenomenon Y”
  • Methods are chosen to test specific hypotheses
  • Results reveal mechanism

Excellent integration:

  • “Understanding X from field A reveals unexpected insight into Y from field B”
  • Creates new frameworks that transcend original disciplines
  • Opens new research directions

The Bigger Picture

Why integration matters for science:

Reductionism Has Limits

  • Breaking systems into parts is powerful
  • But function emerges from interactions
  • Need integrative approaches to understand emergence

Real-World Problems Are Multidisciplinary

  • Disease involves multiple scales (molecular ‚Üí cellular ‚Üí organismal)
  • Effective solutions require integrated understanding
  • Drug development demands chemistry + biology + medicine

Future of Biology

  • Increasingly quantitative
  • Increasingly dependent on technology
  • Increasingly interdisciplinary

Researchers who can integrate across boundaries will drive the next generation of discoveries.

Continuous Learning

My commitment:

  • Stay current in multiple fields
  • Attend diverse conferences
  • Engage with different scientific communities
  • Remain open to new perspectives

Because the most exciting science happens at the intersections.
